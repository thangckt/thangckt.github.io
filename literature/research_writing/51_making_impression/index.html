    Making a good first impression with your paper¶

    Introduction¶

    After many months of effort for writing a reasearch paper, we are ready to submit the outcome to a journal. We then would expect it to be considered seriously by the journal.

    Now put yourself in the place of a journal editor whose daily job involves looking at submissions from authors and deciding on the next steps. Your submission might be one of several that have been received on that particular day.

    Your paper is likely to make a good first impression if

    1. The information in the paper is well-organised;
    2. The scientific content is accurate;
    3. The language is clear; and
    4. You have followed the journal's instructions for authors word-for-word.

    Note

    The 'you' here means you and your co-authors.

    Checking your paper¶

    Before submitting your paper to your target journal, take some time to consider the questions below. This is not an exhaustive list of questions -- you may have to consider more questions depending on your journal's instructions for authors.

    1. Does your paper comply fully with the instructions for authors?
    2. Does your paper contain everything it should? Does it contain anything it shouldn’t?
    3. Is all the information accurate?
      • Have you double-checked the raw data with your records or lab notebook?
      • Have you double-checked the analyses?
    4. Is the content consistent throughout?
      • If you have mentioned a numerical data item more than once, do all the instances match?
    5. Is everything logically organised, clearly worded, and concise? Are grammar, spelling, punctuation, and word use correct throughout?
      • If you find it difficult to write clearly in English, have you sought feedback on the language aspects of your paper?
    6. Are all the figures and tables well designed, numbered, and referred to in the text in the correct sequence?
    7. Are all the abbreviations in the text expanded at their first instance?
    8. Are the citations and references in the correct format?

    Warning

    Do not rush off your paper to your target journal without doing these checks!

    English language editing¶

    English language editor is often called a copy-editor or authors' editor.

    Quote

    The goal of author editing is to help authors produce a clear, accurate and effective document that meets readers' expectations and that will be favorably received by publishers, journal editors and peer reviewers. Therefore, authors' editors do both linguistic editing and substantive editing (editing of "substance", i.e. content). They improve format, structure, grammar, style, data presentation, argumentation, flow and accuracy - Wikipedia

    Preparing a cover letter¶

    A cover letter is normally required along with the paper, as a part of the submission package. Cover letter should include: - A summary of your paper's contribution to the scientific literature - A mention of the type of article you are submitting (for example, research article, systematic review, meta-analysis, clinical trial) - Names of Academic Editors who might be qualified to handle your manuscript

    Researcher identifiers¶

    People often don't have unique names: more than one person may have the same name. Your passport or any other identification document probably has a numeric or alphanumeric identifier in addition to your name and other details. This identifier makes the document unique and establishes your identity.

    As more researchers enter the scholarly record with their publications, it is becoming increasingly difficult to distinguish authors based on just their names. Researcher identifiers help to address this problem. Some of the notable researcher identifiers:

    • ORCID iD
    • Scopus Author ID
    • ResearcherID

    Note on Google Scholar profiles

    Google Scholar is not only an academic database -- you can create a Google Scholar profile for yourself with a list of your publications. One of the most important benefits of having a Google Scholar profile is that you can easily track citations to your papers and see your h-index.

    However, a Google Scholar profile is not a researcher identifier, so it is different from the purpose served by ORCID.

    Submitting paper¶

    Most scientific journals use an online submission process that is part of their manuscript handling system. Such a system helps journal editors handle the workflow of a manuscript, such as receiving a paper, organising peer review, and communicating the decision to authors.

    Common manuscript handling systems include the following:

    • Open Journal Systems
    • ScholarOne Manuscripts
    • Editorial Manager

    Who should submit the paper?

    That is the corresponding author. The corresponding author should make sure that the paper is submitted properly along with any supporting material that's needed. Further, this author should check his/her email regularly and quickly attend to any queries from the journal staff about the paper. The corresponding author is the one who will be notified about the decision on the paper and receive the peer reviewers' comments (via the journal editor).

    Different editorial roles¶

    Kinds of editors in scholarly publishing:

    • Editors who are concerned with the quality of the content in papers (these editors are commonly referred to as journal editors)
      • Possible designations include editor-in-chief, academic editor, scientific editor, senior editor, associate editor
    • Editors who are concerned mainly with the administration of the journal
      • Possible designations include managing editor and production editor
    • Editors who are concerned with the presentation of the content in papers
      • Possible designations include manuscript editor and copy-editor (however, it is usually outside the remit of a journal-side copy-editor's role to assist authors with language improvement; instead authors who need copy-editing support typically have to seek out a freelance copy-editor or authors' editor before they submit their paper to a journal)

    Suggesting peer reviewers¶

    You may be asked to suggest potential peer reviewers for your paper at the time of submission. If you suggest appropriate peer reviewers, you might speed up the process of peer review and receive high-quality reviews for your paper.

    • An appropriate peer reviewer: experienced researcher in your subject area with whom you do not have a personal relationship and who did not play any role in your research project.
    • You might know of them through their publications and you might even have cited their work. You do not need to seek permission from them before suggesting their names. The journal editor will ask them if they can review your paper if the editor thinks they would make suitable peer reviewers. (Note that journal editors are not obliged to use the reviewers you have suggested.)
    • It is important to avoid any conflict of interest when suggesting potential peer reviewers. Do not recommend your own research colleagues, supervisors, or anyone else who may have a bias (positive or negative).
    • Remember to provide the email addresses of the reviewers you suggest, and these should ideally be institutional email addresses.

    Suggesting reviewers to avoid

    Some research groups compete with one another, and they may not want their papers to be reviewed by researchers from a competing group.

    Your target journal may give you the option of suggesting the names of researchers who should not review your paper. Think carefully before suggesting any names here, and do it only if you are fairly sure that someone will be biased against your work.
