# Writing a research paper

The expected structure can vary from one journal to another even within the same subject area. Most search papers follow a typical structure:

- Title
- Authors
- Abstract
- Introduction
- Methods
- Results
- Discussion (sometimes followed by a Conclusion section)
- Acknowledgements
- References
- Appendices (occasionally)

!!! note
    The Results and Discussion sections are not always separate; they may be combined and appear as one section in some journals.

The body structure is known as IMRAD (IMRD, IMRaD), which stands for Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion.

- Introduction: What is the research problem or question?
- Methods: What did you do to find the answer?
- Results: What did you find?
- Discussion: What do your findings mean?

## Title of paper

- Should clearly convey the scope of the research reported in the paper.
- Should be written without abbreviations or acronyms if possible.
- Avoid using a phrase like 'A study of', 'Observations on' or 'analysis of' at the beginning. These are empty phrases and contain no useful information.

## Abstract

Researchers generally read the abstract of a paper before deciding whether to read the entire paper. Also, journal editors and peer reviewers get their first impression of a paper from its abstract.

- The abstract should usually contain information from each section of the paper. It should be organised in a sort of mini IMRAD format without references (although there are some exceptions to this rule).
- Consider to answer these questions:
    - Why did you do the study? 
    - What did you do? 
    - What did you find? 
    - What did you conclude?

## Introduction

The Introduction section has two purposes.

- The first purpose is to provide a background to the research work. The introduction should aim to help readers understand the paper and appreciate the importance of the research.
- The second purpose is to identify the question(s) addressed by the research. The research questions are sometimes stated as hypotheses. It is a good recommendation to explicitly state your research questions in the Introduction.

The Introduction section is analogous to a funnel. At the beginning, there is a considerable amount of general information. Slowly, the focus narrows and the information becomes specific. The following information should appear in order

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Kitchen_Funnel.jpg/640px-Kitchen_Funnel.jpg)

- First, broad background and context
- Second, review of reelvant literatures
- Third, introduction to the gaps or unknowns you have addressed
- Fourth, explanation of why these gaps are important
- Last, your research question or hypotheses

Consider the following questions:
1. What is the problem that the paper is solving 
2. Why that problem (the importance of the problem) 
3. What are the current approaches to solving this problem and what is wrong about them
4. What are the contributions of the paper

## Methods

The Methods section can appear with other names. Common headings for this section include the following: Methods, Materials and methods, Experimental details, Methodology, Simulations, or Computational setup.

The Methods section is essentially a detailed answer to this: 'What did you do to find the answers to your research questions?'

The following information should be included in the Method section:
- Details of any equipment, organisms, reagents, etc. used
- Names of the manufacturers from which equipment or materials were obtained
- Details of approval of human or animal research by an appropriate committee
- Types of statistical and/or computational procedures used
- Tables with information on experimental or observational conditions

**Credibility and reproducibility**

The Methods section is the foundation of a scientific paper. Journal editors and peer reviewers usually inspect the Methods section closely to check if the study is methodologically sound. When reviewing your paper, journal editors and peer reviewers will ascertain whether...

- your Methods section is complete and clear
- your Methods can be followed by a researcher in the future to repeat or build upon your work

The Methods section provides credibility to a scientific paper.

## Results 

This section present the findings or outcomes of the research study. Unless the Results are part of a section called 'Results and Discussion', only the findings should be presented in the Results section, not explanations of the findings

## Discussion

The basic purpose of the Discussion section is to explain the results of your research. In some papers, there may be a short Conclusions section after the Discussion section.

1. Explaining the meaning of your findings
2. Clearly answering the research questions or hypotheses you had stated in your Introduction section
3. Acknowledging any limitations of your study, and their possible influence on the results
4. Clarifying how your findings are related to the findings of any similar studies done previously
5. Suggesting the implications of your research study for researchers, practitioners, or policymakers
6. Suggesting themes for future research based on what you have found

Earlier in this lesson, we saw that the Introduction should be shaped like a funnel (broad to narrow): starting with the big picture of the research topic and ending with the research questions of the study.

The Discussion is structured in the opposite way (narrow to broad): starting with an analysis of the results of the study and ending with suggestions for future research or practice.

## Acknowledgements

To thank the people who have helped you with your research in some way.

If your work was supported by a grant, mention the grant number, if any, and the funding agency.
